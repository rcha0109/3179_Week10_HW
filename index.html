The Unequal Reality of Maternal Health

Uncovering maternal mortality ratios (MMR) across regions and countries.

The maternal mortality ratio (MMR) is the number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births. High MMR reflects gender gaps in healthcare and autonomy. It signals inequities in education, resources, and societal decision-making.

Climbing Uneven Ladders

How women’s share in leadership trails behind their overall workforce presence.

The Drop to the Top

How women’s representation shrinks from workforce to leadership across industries.

Physical norms reinforce gender stereotypes

Many women start the climb, but few reach the top. They dominate Health Care, Education, and Retail — sectors shaped by traditional caregiving roles. Even in these fields, barriers still hinder leadership advancement.

By contrast, leadership gaps remain wide in Construction, Oil, and Transportation. Male-dominated cultures and rigid structures limit women’s advancement.

The Learning Curve of Inequality

Investigating gender gaps in adult literacy across global income groups.

Women in low-income regions face steep literacy disadvantages versus men. Middle- and high-income countries narrow gaps, yet disparities remain.

Limited education access reinforces stereotypes, constraining empowerment and autonomy. It entrenches social norms, limiting career and leadership opportunities.

What Holds Equality Back?

A cross-country examination of perceived obstacles to gender equality.

Click an obstacle to highlight it on the charts.

Conflict increases women’s vulnerability in Yemen.

In very high HDI regions, unequal responsibilities dominate. Formal barriers fade, yet deep-rooted norms sustain gendered divisions of unpaid labour across households.

In high HDI countries, workplace inequality persists despite strong education access. Policy gaps like limited childcare and parental leave imbalance reinforce disadvantage.

Conversely, in lower HDI regions, religion and culture remain dominant barriers. Enduring social norms restrict women’s education and participation, reinforcing dependence.

Interestingly, only the U.S. cited "different treatment growing up." Americans view inequality as rooted in early socialisation.
